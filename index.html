How county budgets reflect public priorities in MN:

Visualizing spending on criminal justice in the state where George Floyd was murdered

Authors: Nathan Arnosti, Laier-Rayshon Smith, & Madeline Wrable


George Floyd's murder at the hands of the Minneapolis Police Department on May 25, 2020 ignited the largest mass protests in U.S. history - and a national public conversation about policing, public safety, and racial justice.

Protests against police brutality and an unjust criminal justice system call attention to the many ways that public funds, and public institutions, inflict harm on people in systemic, racialized patterns. This raises a profound question: how should public dollars in a just society be spent?

Why budgets?

Budgets are moral documents, reflective of the values and preferences of those who set them. We are visualizing the budgets of all 87 counties in Minnesota to understand those values. Pairing budget data with demographic data, we explore how percent spending on policing varies by racial composition per county.


Why counties?

While public attention has largely focused on policing in the city of Minneapolis in the wake of George Floyd’s murder, a statewide conversation about policing is necessary. Nearly 70% of police-involved deaths since 2001 have occurred outside of the Twin Cities of Minneapolis and Saint Paul, according to a recent study by the Star Tribune, Minnesota’s paper of record.

Research by the Urban Institute reveals that every level of government - including cities, counties, states, and the federal government - spends money on policing in various forms. This analysis focused on county governments, which fund police and sheriff services, courts, and county jails, as well as critical safety net services to residents including mental health, chemical dependency, child protection, and disability supports.



























Scatterplot Results

Percent budget spent on policing:

In 2000, nearly every county spent less than 20% on policing, and many spent between 5-10%. By 2018, all counties were below 25% spent on policing, and many spent between 7.5-15%. In summary, a noticeable increase in police spending was visible over time.


Percent BIPOC residents:

In 2000, most counties had less than 25% BIPOC residents, with a short range of 1-25%. By 2018, most counties had less than 40% BIPOC residents, with a wide range of 4-40%. In summary, the percent BIPOC residents increased over time as well as the range of values.

Methodology and data sources: This is a recreation of a visualization made famous by Hans Roling and adapted by Mike Bostock. Police spending data comes from the Minnesota State Auditor’s Minnesota County Finances Reports from 2000 to 2018. This analysis visualizes current expenditures only, and does not include capital expenditures. Due to missing county population data for the years 2001 - 2003 and 2005, police spending percentages for those years have been estimated using linear interpolation. Racial demographic data comes from the 2000 and 2010 Census and from the 2018 5-year ACS. It has been interpolated across intervening years.
