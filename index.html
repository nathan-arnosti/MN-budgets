How county budgets reflect public priorities in MN:

Visualizing spending on criminal justice in the state where George Floyd was murdered

Authors: Nathan Arnosti, Laier-Rayshon Smith, & Madeline Wrable


George Floyd's murder at the hands of the Minneapolis Police Department on May 25, 2020 ignited the largest mass protests in U.S. history - and a national public conversation about policing, public safety, and racial justice.

Protests against police brutality and an unjust criminal justice system call attention to the many ways that public funds, and public institutions, inflict harm on people in systemic, racialized patterns. This raises a profound question: how should public dollars in a just society be spent?

Why study budgets?

Budgets are moral documents, reflective of the values and preferences of those who set them. We are visualizing the budgets of all 87 counties in Minnesota to understand those values. Pairing budget data with demographic data from the U.S. Census and American Community Survey, we explore how percent spending on policing services varies by racial composition per county.


Why study counties?

While public attention has largely focused on policing in the city of Minneapolis in the wake of George Floyd’s murder, a statewide conversation about policing is necessary. Nearly 70% of police-involved deaths since 2001 have occurred outside of the Twin Cities of Minneapolis and Saint Paul, according to a recent study by the Star Tribune, Minnesota’s paper of record.

Research by the Urban Institute reveals that every level of government - including cities, counties, states, and the federal government - spends money on policing in various forms. This analysis focused on county governments, which play a particularly important role in funding criminal justice expenditures such as sheriffs offices, courts, county jails, and correctional facilities, as well as critical safety net services to residents including mental health, chemical dependency, child protection, and disability supports. Our project focuses on sheriff expenditures, which we also refer to as “policing.”


Why use a scatterplot?

In order to visualize our variables, police spending as compared to racial composition over time, we are using an animated scatterplot. This scatterplot displays Minnesota counties (n = 87), where the percentage of police spending out of the total county expenditures is on the y-axis and the percentage of Black, Indigenous, or People of Color (BIPOC) residents out of the total population is on the x-axis. The scatterplot is animated through time from 2000-2018. Counties are sized by population and color-coded by geographic region: the “Core Counties” of Hennepin and Ramsey, where Minneapolis and Saint Paul are located; the five counties of the “Twin Cities suburbs”; and “Greater Minnesota.”




In 2000, many counties spent between 5% and 10% of their budgets on policing services, and hardly any spent more than 20%. By 2018, spending on policing increased somewhat across most counties. Nearly all counties spent at least 7.5% of their budgets on policing, and most spent between 10% and 25%.

Increased police expenditures coincided with increasing racial diversity among county residents. In 2000, fewer than 10% of residents in all counties in the Twin Cities suburbs and most in Greater Minnesota were BIPOC. Roughly 1 in 4 residents of the Core Counties were BIPOC. By 2018, BIPOC residents comprised at least 5% of the population in nearly every county, roughly 15% to 20% in the Twin Cities suburbs, and close to 1 in 3 in the Core Counties.

There appears to be no strong link between the racial demographics of the county, its population, and its spending on policing. The two most populous counties in the state, the Core Counties, spent a smaller percentage of their budget on policing than most counties in Greater Minnesota. Still, it’s worth noting that while racial demographics do not appear to predict county policing spending, counties in Minnesota are becoming both more diverse and spending somewhat more on policing services.

Changes in policing budgets & BIPOC residents by county, 2000 - 2018

The following visualization shows percentage point increases across policing budgets & share of BIPOC residents from 2000 to 2018. A county whose spending on policing increased from 10% in 2000 to 15% in 2018 would show a percentage point increase of +5. When looking at change over time since 2000, the underlying trends reveal themselves. Nearly all of Minnesota's counties are becoming more racially diverse. And larger shares of county budgets are going towards policing.

Methodology and data sources: This is a recreation of a visualization made famous by Hans Roling and adapted by Mike Bostock. Police spending data comes from the Minnesota State Auditor’s Minnesota County Finances Reports from 2000 to 2018. This analysis visualizes current expenditures only, and does not include capital expenditures. Due to missing county population data for the years 2001 - 2003 and 2005, police spending percentages for those years have been estimated using linear interpolation. Racial demographic data comes from the 2000 and 2010 Census and from the 2018 5-year ACS. It has been interpolated across intervening years.
